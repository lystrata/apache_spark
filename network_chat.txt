Question:
We have are bringing up an Apache Spark on-prem infrastructure that will batch load parquet data one a day to a remote Snowflake instance on Azure. Our input to Spark will be approximately 2Tb of CSV data per day. I know that the conversion to Parquet will reduce the overall size, but I do not know by how much. Once this data is assembled on our S3 data store, we will send it to Snowflake using the Spark to Snowflake connector. Is this connector transfer a monolithic stream form one outbound port to on inbound port, or is it broken into multiple streans?

The Snowflake Spark connector transfer is not a monolithic stream; it is a distributed, multi-step process that leverages Spark's parallel architecture to break data into multiple streams and files. [1, 2, 3, 4]
Data Transfer Mechanism
The connector facilitates transfer through two primary stages: [4, 5]
Distributed Staging (Parallel Write): Spark's distributed execution engine writes the dataset as multiple files into a temporary cloud storage location (an internal or external stage). In your case, this will be your S3 bucket (for AWS) or Azure Blob storage.
Bulk Loading (COPY INTO): Once the files are staged, the connector issues a COPY INTO command to Snowflake. Snowflake’s virtual warehouse then reads these files in parallel from the cloud storage into the target table. [1, 2, 6, 7, 8]
Key Performance Details
Parallelism: Because Spark is a distributed system, multiple Spark executors write files to S3 simultaneously, creating multiple outbound streams rather than one single pipe.
Throughput: This staged approach provides significantly better throughput than a single-stream JDBC connection, as it allows Snowflake to utilize the full compute power of its virtual warehouse to ingest multiple files at once.
Connectivity on Azure: Since your Snowflake instance is on Azure, your network traffic remains within the Azure network and does not traverse the public internet, provided the configuration is correct. [2, 9, 10, 11, 12]
Parquet Conversion Impact
While you mentioned 2TB of CSV data, converting to Parquet will significantly reduce this size—often by 3x to 10x depending on data types and redundancy—due to its columnar storage and compression algorithms. Snowflake can then use its vectorized scanner for Parquet to further improve loading speeds by up to 35%. [7, 13]

Question: 
In our current working plan, we will have 4 Spark Workers. Does that mean we would have 4 parallel streams?

Not necessarily. While you have 4 Workers, the number of parallel outbound streams is actually determined by the number of Spark Partitions and Executors, not just the physical worker count. [1, 2]
In Spark's distributed architecture, each worker can run one or more executors, and each executor can process multiple tasks in parallel if it has multiple CPU cores. [3, 4]
How Parallelism is Determined
Partitions = Streams: Every partition in your Spark DataFrame represents a separate task. When the Snowflake connector writes to S3, each task creates its own file and its own outbound stream to your storage bucket.
Executor Slots: If your 4 workers each have an executor with 4 cores, you could potentially have 16 parallel streams (4 workers × 4 cores) writing to S3 at once.
Saturation Rule: To maximize your 4 workers, you generally want at least 2–3 partitions per available CPU core to ensure no worker sits idle while others are finishing their tasks. [2, 4, 5, 6, 7]
The Two Phases of Your Transfer
Outbound (Spark to S3): This is highly parallel. If your 2TB of data is split into 1,000 partitions, Spark will stream those partitions to S3 using as many "slots" (cores) as your 4 workers provide.
Inbound (S3 to Snowflake): Once Spark finishes writing the files, it triggers a COPY INTO command. At this stage, the parallelism shifts to Snowflake's Virtual Warehouse. A larger Snowflake warehouse (e.g., Large vs. Small) can ingest more of those S3 files simultaneously. [2, 5, 8, 9, 10]
Optimization Tip
For 2TB of daily data, ensure your Spark partitions result in files between 100MB and 250MB (compressed). If your files are too small, you'll hit "small file" overhead; if they are too large (e.g., many GBs), Snowflake won't be able to parallelize the final load efficiently. [2, 5, 11]

Question:
I think each worker will have 24 cores allocated to it. Can you discuss exactly what an "executor" is in this context?

In the Spark world, an Executor is the actual "worker bee" process that lives inside your physical Worker machine.
Think of the Worker as the hardware (the truck) and the Executor as the engine and crew inside it doing the heavy lifting.
The Breakdown for Your 96-Core Cluster
With 4 Workers and 24 cores each, you have 96 total cores. Here is how Executors fit in:
The Process: An Executor is a dedicated Java Virtual Machine (JVM) process. It stays alive for the duration of your Spark application to run tasks and store data in memory.
The "Slots" (Cores): Each core allocated to an executor acts as a task slot. If you assign all 24 cores to one massive executor on a worker, that executor can run 24 tasks (and thus 24 parallel outbound streams) simultaneously.
The Strategy: You usually don't want one giant 24-core executor because JVM overhead (like Garbage Collection) can get sluggish. A common "sweet spot" is 5 cores per executor.
Your Parallel Stream Potential
If you configure your 4 Workers with four 5-core executors each:
Total Executors: 16 (4 workers × 4 executors).
Total Parallel Streams: 80 (16 executors × 5 cores).
In this scenario, your Spark cluster would be pushing 80 simultaneous streams of Parquet data to your S3/Azure storage.
Why This Matters for Your 2TB Load
Since you are batch-loading 2TB daily, the "Executor" configuration dictates your outbound throughput. If you leave it at default settings, Spark might only spin up one executor per worker, which could underutilize those 24 cores.
By tuning the number of executors and cores, you ensure that all 96 cores are actively "streaming" data out to your storage stage at the same time, significantly cutting down that daily batch window.

For a 2TB daily batch load, the goal is to balance the massive amount of data across your 96 available cores while ensuring the resulting files are the "perfect" size for Snowflake to ingest efficiently.
The Strategy
Target File Size: Snowflake performs best when files are between 100MB and 250MB (compressed).
Partition Count: If 2TB of CSV compresses down to ~400GB of Parquet (a conservative 5x reduction), you want roughly 1,600 to 2,000 partitions (400GB / 250MB).
Parallelism: With 96 cores, 2,000 partitions means each core will handle about 20 small "tasks" sequentially, keeping your outbound pipes fully saturated.
Sample Spark Configuration
You can set these values when initializing your SparkSession or via spark-submit flags.
from pyspark.sql import SparkSession

spark = SparkSession.builder \
    .appName("2TB_Snowflake_Load") \
    # 1. Executor Strategy: 4 Workers * 4 Executors each = 16 Executors
    .config("spark.executor.instances", "16") \
    # 2. 5 cores per executor is the "sweet spot" for I/O and GC
    .config("spark.executor.cores", "5") \
    # 3. Handle 2TB shuffles; default 200 is way too low
    .config("spark.sql.shuffle.partitions", "2000") \
    # 4. Maximize read speed from your on-prem source
    .config("spark.sql.files.maxPartitionBytes", "128mb") \
    .getOrCreate()

# Before writing to Snowflake, force the partition count
df = spark.read.csv("s3://your-on-prem-source/2TB_data/")

# repartition(2000) creates exactly 2000 parallel streams to S3
df.repartition(2000).write \
    .format("net.snowflake.spark.snowflake") \
    .options(**snowflake_options) \
    .mode("append") \
    .save()
Why this works
spark.executor.cores=5: Restricting each executor to 5 cores (instead of all 24) prevents "fat executors" from suffering long Garbage Collection pauses, which can stall your 2TB stream.
repartition(2000): This is the "volume knob." It tells Spark to divide the data into 2,000 chunks. Because you have 80 active task slots (16 executors × 5 cores), Spark will push 80 files at a time to S3 until all 2,000 are done.
Snowflake Ingestion: When Spark finishes, it tells Snowflake to COPY INTO from S3. Snowflake will see 2,000 perfectly-sized 200MB files and can use its own parallel nodes to suck that data in at maximum speed.

Question:

We have 10 GBs to our EDGE, after that it is(currently) a 1GB link to Azure. We are trying to determine if this is adequate, or if we need to increase it to 10GB

A 1Gbps link is theoretically enough to move 2TB within a day, but it leaves you very little "breathing room" for errors, re-runs, or future growth.
The Math (2TB daily load)
Assuming your 2TB of CSV compresses to roughly 400GB of Parquet (5x compression):
Link Speed	Theoretical Time	Realistic Time (80% Utility)
1 Gbps	~54 Minutes	~1.2 Hours
10 Gbps	~6 Minutes	~8 Minutes
At first glance, 1.2 hours seems fine for a once-a-day batch. However, there are two major reasons to consider the 10GB upgrade now:
1. The "Re-Run" Window
If a batch fails at 10 PM and you have to re-run 2TB of data, a 1Gbps link means you are waiting over an hour just for the data to travel. In a production environment, you typically want your "transfer window" to be as small as possible to allow for multiple retry attempts without missing your morning SLA (Service Level Agreement).
2. Network Saturation & "Micro-bursting"
Because you have 80 parallel streams (16 executors × 5 cores) capable of pushing data at 10Gbps locally to your edge, they will instantly saturate a 1Gbps outbound link.
The Risk: This saturation creates "buffer bloat" at your edge router. While Spark handles this, it can lead to high latency for other services sharing that same 1Gbps link (like RDP sessions, VPNs, or other cloud apps).
The 10GB Advantage: A 10Gbps link matches your internal speed, allowing Spark to "burst" the data to Azure as fast as the workers can process it, then get off the wire quickly.
Recommendation
If this 2TB is business-critical and expected to grow: Upgrade to 10GB.
If this is a pilot project and you can tolerate a 2-hour delay if a failure occurs: Stay with 1GB for now, but monitor your edge router's interface discards and latency during the load.
