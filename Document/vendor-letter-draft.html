Subject: Development Cluster Sizing Analysis — Basis for Discussion

Over the past several weeks, our team has been conducting a structured self-assessment of the hardware and software interactions involved in standing up an Apache Spark cluster on our existing 3-node HPE DL385 Gen11 infrastructure. The attached documents represent that work. We are sharing them ahead of our technical discussion so that your team has full visibility into our current thinking — and so that you can correct, refine, or validate it against your deployment experience.

What we set out to answer

We approached this from two practical needs rather than from a theoretical interest in Spark:

First, we need to be able to give our development team realistic, defensible guidelines for how they interact with the cluster — specifically around ingest data volume, shuffle complexity, and the job patterns that will stay within the hardware envelope versus those that will exhaust it. A developer who doesn't understand why their query failed, or who is handed an arbitrary limit without explanation, is a friction point we want to avoid from the start.

Second, we need to be able to justify hardware upgrade requests internally with something more rigorous than "we need more capacity." The analysis needs to trace from a specific workload characteristic — shuffle amplification, concurrent Parquet accumulation, OSD count — through to a concrete impact on cluster headroom or job reliability. That traceability is what gives an upgrade request credibility with budget stakeholders who are not Spark practitioners.

What the documents contain

The four attached documents cover the storage architecture analysis, the development cluster sizing calculator, the underlying sizing mathematics, and a general-purpose sizing tool for exploring configurations beyond the current hardware. They are not presented as authoritative — they are presented as our current best understanding, derived from first principles and the available literature, by a team that has not yet deployed Spark in production.

One correction relative to any documents shared prior to this submission: earlier versions of the storage reference and sizing calculator assumed worst-case shuffle amplification without accounting for Spark's default shuffle write compression (snappy codec, enabled by default). Our subsequent review of the shuffle mechanics literature confirmed that this compression is active unless explicitly disabled, and typically reduces on-disk shuffle volume by 40–60% relative to the raw data size. The attached documents have been updated to reflect this — the scratch sizing model remains intentionally conservative (it plans to raw data volume), but the updated materials now annotate that the effective scratch headroom is approximately twice what the displayed ceiling suggests when default compression is active.

What we are looking for from this discussion

Your team has real-world deployment experience that we do not. Where our assumptions are wrong, conservative to the point of being impractical, or missing factors that only surface in production, we want to know. Specifically, we are interested in whether our shuffle amplification estimates, OSD configuration reasoning, and scratch sizing approach reflect what you observe in comparable deployments — and where the meaningful gaps are between a sizing model and an actual running cluster.

We are not coming to this conversation to defend a position. We are coming to it to pressure-test an analysis against people who have done this before, so that the decisions we make about hardware, developer guidelines, and upgrade justification are grounded in something real.
